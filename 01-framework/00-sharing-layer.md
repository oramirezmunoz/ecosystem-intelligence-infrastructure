# Layer 0: The Sharing Layer

## Why This Layer Comes First

The sharing layer is not one of six equal components of EII. 
It is the prerequisite for all of them.

Without sharing, there is nothing to collect. Without collection, 
there is nothing to aggregate. Without aggregation, there is nothing 
to translate or distribute. The entire infrastructure depends on 
this layer functioning — which means building EII always begins 
here, not with technology or platforms, but with the conditions 
that make sharing possible.

This is also why most attempts at ecosystem information systems 
fail. They invest in aggregation and distribution before they 
have earned the sharing that makes both possible.

---

## The Foundational Principle: Data Sovereignty

The sharing layer is built on one non-negotiable principle:

> **Data, content, and information always belong to the 
> organisations and individuals who created or hold it. 
> Ecosystem actors are signal contributors — not data 
> sources. They decide what to share, in what form, 
> with whom, and under what conditions. Always.**

This is not just an ethical position. It is a design requirement.

EII does not centralise data. It does not extract data. It does 
not hold data on behalf of anyone. It operates on a federated 
model — data stays with its rightful owner, and only the signals 
and insights that owners choose to contribute flow through the 
infrastructure.

This principle has three immediate consequences:

**1. GDPR and regulatory compliance become design features, 
not barriers.**
When data never leaves its owner, compliance is built in from 
the start. The sharing layer must be designed to make this 
explicit — clear consent frameworks, defined data usage rules, 
and audit trails for every signal contributed.

**2. Trust becomes achievable.**
Actors are far more willing to share when they retain full 
control. The sovereignty principle removes the biggest 
psychological barrier to participation — the fear of losing 
control over sensitive information.

**3. The infrastructure is more resilient.**
A federated model has no single point of failure. If one 
actor stops sharing, the rest of the infrastructure continues 
to function.

---

## The Federated Model

EII adopts a federated architecture for the sharing layer, 
directly inspired by the most advanced global approaches 
to multi-stakeholder data sharing — including the European 
Data Spaces model developed under the GAIA-X initiative.

In a federated model:

- Data stays with its owner, stored in their own systems
- Owners define what signals they are willing to contribute 
  and under what conditions
- Contributed signals flow through the infrastructure 
  according to agreed governance rules
- The neutral operator manages the infrastructure but 
  never owns the data
- Every sharing relationship is governed by explicit, 
  auditable agreements

This is fundamentally different from a centralised platform 
model — where data is uploaded, stored centrally, and 
controlled by the platform operator. That model creates 
exactly the trust and sovereignty concerns that prevent 
actors from sharing in the first place.

## Why Sharing Is Hard: The Real Barriers

### Trust

Trust is the primary barrier — and the most frequently 
underestimated one. Actors will not share information with 
a system or operator they do not trust, regardless of how 
good the technology is or how compelling the value 
proposition appears.

Trust cannot be mandated. It cannot be built overnight. 
It must be earned — through demonstrated behaviour over 
time, through governance that gives actors real control, 
and through intermediaries who already have established 
relationships with the actors they are asking to share.

### Regulatory complexity

GDPR and equivalent data protection regulations create 
real constraints — particularly for actors handling 
personal data (talent profiles, individual entrepreneur 
data, employee information). These constraints are 
manageable by design, but they must be addressed 
explicitly in the governance of the sharing layer, 
not treated as an afterthought.

### Legacy systems

Many ecosystem actors — particularly public institutions, 
universities, and large corporates — operate on legacy 
IT systems that were never designed for interoperability. 
Connecting these systems to any shared infrastructure 
requires technical bridging that takes time and resources.

### Institutional inertia

Sharing requires someone inside an organisation to 
champion it, navigate internal approvals, and invest 
time in a process whose benefits may not be immediately 
visible to their own organisation. In large or bureaucratic 
organisations this is a significant barrier — not because 
of bad intentions, but because of how institutions 
allocate attention and resources.

### No perceived return

The most common reason actors don't share is simple: 
they don't see what they get in return. Sharing feels 
like giving something away. The sharing layer must be 
designed so that contributing a signal always produces 
visible, tangible value for the contributor.

## What Makes Sharing Possible: Design Principles

### 1. Sovereignty by design
Every element of the sharing layer must reinforce 
the data sovereignty principle. Actors must be able 
to see exactly what they have shared, who has accessed 
it, for what purpose, and with what result. Control 
must be real, not just promised.

### 2. Reciprocity as the core incentive
Every act of sharing must produce a return. This 
return does not have to be immediate or monetary — 
it can be access to insights, better connections, 
improved visibility, or more relevant support. But 
it must be visible and attributable to the act 
of sharing. The infrastructure must be designed 
so that contributing actors receive more value 
than non-contributing actors.

### 3. Actor-specific sharing models
There is no universal sharing model. Each actor 
type has different data sensitivities, different 
regulatory constraints, different incentive 
structures, and different levels of technical 
capacity. The sharing layer must accommodate all 
of them — with different consent mechanisms, 
different levels of anonymisation, and different 
governance rules for different actor types.

Refer to the [Actor Taxonomy](../00-foundation/actor-taxonomy.md) 
for the specific sharing unlocks for each actor type.

### 4. Anonymisation extends reach
Many signals that actors are unwilling to share 
at the individual or organisational level can be 
shared safely in aggregate — contributing to 
the ecosystem picture without exposing sensitive 
information. The sharing layer must support 
multiple levels of signal granularity:

- **Identified signals** — shared with explicit 
  consent for specific uses (e.g. a corporate 
  announcing openness to startup pilots)
- **Anonymised signals** — shared at category 
  level without identifying the contributor 
  (e.g. "a large company in this sector is 
  looking for solutions in this area")
- **Aggregate signals** — statistical patterns 
  derived from multiple contributors without 
  attributing any individual signal 
  (e.g. "40% of corporates in this ecosystem 
  report an unmet need for X")

### 5. Trusted intermediaries over cold systems
Sharing happens through trusted relationships — 
not through platforms that ask actors to contribute 
data to a system they have no relationship with. 
The sharing layer must be built through existing 
trust networks — ecosystem operators, industry 
associations, chambers of commerce, and other 
intermediaries who already have established 
relationships with the actors they are 
asking to share.

### 6. Compliance by design
GDPR and equivalent regulations must be embedded 
in the governance of the sharing layer from the 
start — not bolted on afterwards. This means:

- Explicit consent mechanisms for every 
  type of signal shared
- Clear data usage agreements between 
  the neutral operator and all contributors
- Defined retention periods and deletion 
  rights for all signals
- Audit trails for every sharing transaction
- Privacy impact assessments before any 
  new signal type is introduced

### 7. Legacy system accommodation
The sharing layer must meet actors where they 
are technically — not require them to replace 
their existing systems. This means designing 
lightweight integration mechanisms (APIs, 
structured forms, periodic data exports) that 
work with legacy infrastructure rather than 
demanding migration to new systems.

## The Neutral Operator

The sharing layer requires a designated neutral 
operator — an organisation or body that:

- Has no competitive interest in any of the 
  signals it manages
- Is trusted by all actor types in the ecosystem
- Has a formal, permanent mandate to operate 
  the infrastructure
- Is governed by a multi-stakeholder board 
  that represents the interests of contributing 
  actors
- Is sustainably funded — not through project 
  grants that expire, but through a permanent 
  funding model

This entity does not currently exist in most 
ecosystems. Creating it — with the right mandate, 
governance, and funding — is the first act of 
building EII. Everything else depends on it.

The neutral operator is not a technology company. 
It is not a government agency. It is not an 
accelerator or an incubator. It is a purpose-built 
infrastructure operator — closer in nature to a 
standards body or a public utility than to any 
existing category of ecosystem actor.

The full design of the neutral operator is 
developed in the 
[Governance Model](./05-governance-model.md).

## Building the Sharing Layer in Practice

### Phase 1 — Establish the neutral operator
Before any sharing can happen, the operator 
must exist, have a mandate, and have the 
beginning of trust from key actor types. 
This typically requires backing from a 
credible public or institutional actor — 
a development agency, a national innovation 
body, or an equivalent.

### Phase 2 — Start with willing actors
Do not try to get all actor types sharing 
from the start. Begin with the actors who 
have the most to gain and the lowest barriers 
to sharing — typically ecosystem operators 
(accelerators, incubators), entrepreneurial 
associations, and event organisers. Early 
wins build the proof of value that unlocks 
more reluctant actors later.

### Phase 3 — Demonstrate reciprocity visibly
Show early contributors exactly what their 
sharing produced — which connections were 
made, which insights were generated, which 
actors benefited. Make the return on sharing 
concrete and visible as early as possible. 
This is the most powerful trust-building 
mechanism available.

### Phase 4 — Expand actor by actor
Add actor types progressively — moving from 
lowest to highest barrier. Each new actor 
type requires a tailored onboarding approach, 
a specific sharing model, and a clear value 
proposition. Never add a new actor type before 
the sharing model for that type is properly 
designed and governed.

### Phase 5 — Institutionalise
Once the sharing layer has demonstrated value 
across multiple actor types, work to embed 
it in the formal structures of the ecosystem 
— through policy mandates, funding agreements, 
and inter-institutional governance frameworks 
that make participation the default rather 
than the exception.

---

## What the Sharing Layer Is Not

- It is not a data marketplace where signals 
  are bought and sold
- It is not a centralised database owned 
  by any single actor
- It is not a government surveillance system
- It is not a one-time mapping exercise
- It is not optional infrastructure that 
  ecosystems can choose to skip

It is the foundation on which every other 
layer of EII is built. Without it, the rest 
of the framework does not exist.
