# Layer 1: The Collection Layer

## Purpose

The collection layer is the system through which ecosystem 
signals are gathered from the actors who generate them. 
It sits immediately above the sharing layer — once actors 
are willing and able to share, the collection layer 
determines how those signals are captured, at what 
frequency, and in what form.

Getting this layer right is the difference between an 
ecosystem that learns in real time and one that discovers 
what happened six months ago in an annual report.

---

## The Core Principle: Intelligence, Not Reporting

The most consequential design decision in the collection 
layer is the intent behind it.

Most ecosystem operators collect information because a 
donor asked for it, a reporting template required it, 
or a program evaluation needed it. Collection is treated 
as a compliance function — something that happens at 
the beginning and end of a program cycle, in rigid 
templates, from actors already inside their own programs.

This produces survey fatigue, outdated data, siloed 
spreadsheets, and an ecosystem that learns very little 
in real time.

EII treats collection as an intelligence function — 
designed for reuse, learning, and operational action. 
The question is never "what do we need to report?" 
It is always "what do we need to understand, and 
how do we capture it as close to the source as possible?"

That distinction changes everything about how the 
collection layer is designed.

---

## What Gets Collected

Not all signals are equal. The collection layer must 
distinguish between three types:

**Operational signals** — what is happening right now 
in the ecosystem. Founder needs, support requests, 
referral patterns, engagement with services, emerging 
topics. These change fast and lose value quickly 
if not captured in context.

**Structural signals** — the relatively stable 
characteristics of the ecosystem. Actor profiles, 
capability assessments, program inventories, 
policy landscapes. These change slowly and are 
best captured periodically.

**Outcome signals** — what actually resulted from 
ecosystem activity. Partnerships formed, funding 
secured, programs completed, companies scaled. 
These are event-triggered and require systematic 
capture at defined moments.

A common mistake is collecting only structural signals — 
actor counts, program numbers, participation rates — 
while missing operational signals entirely. This produces 
activity data without ecosystem understanding. You can 
measure motion without understanding function.

---

## The Collection Stack

No single collection method captures the full range 
of ecosystem signals. Different signals appear in 
different moments and formats. An effective collection 
layer is a stack of five complementary mechanisms.

---

### 1. Embedded conversational collection

The richest collection method — and the most 
underused. When ecosystem actors are already 
interacting with a program, platform, advisor, 
or AI assistant, signals can be captured naturally 
as part of the exchange.

This is almost always better than asking people 
afterwards what they needed. It captures intent, 
pain points, timing, and nuance in real context — 
not reconstructed from memory.

**What it captures:**
- Founder needs and blockers at the moment 
  they surface
- Support gaps — what actors are asking for 
  that no one provides
- Language people actually use to describe 
  their challenges — essential for translation 
  and communication design
- Hidden relationships and trust issues 
  that never appear in structured data
- Weak signals before they become obvious — 
  often the most valuable category

**Why it matters:**
This is where you discover what your taxonomy 
is missing. Structured collection captures 
what you already know to ask. Conversational 
collection surfaces what you didn't know 
to look for.

**Collection frequency:** Continuous

---

### 2. Structured forms and surveys

Useful for signals you already know you want 
to measure — stage, sector, team size, funding 
status, needs categories, satisfaction, program 
outcomes. The value is comparability: structured 
data can be tracked over time, aggregated across 
actors, and fed directly into dashboards and KPIs.

Their limitation is equally important: they 
capture what you asked, not what is actually 
emerging. A survey designed in January will 
miss the trend that appeared in March.

**What it captures:**
- Baseline actor profiles and classifications
- Program satisfaction and perceived value
- Needs across predefined categories
- Outcome tracking at defined milestones
- Benchmark data for ecosystem health indicators

**Design principles:**
- Capture small signals often rather than 
  comprehensive signals rarely
- Ask only what creates clear value for 
  the actor being asked — actors are not 
  unpaid data clerks
- Reuse data already available rather than 
  asking again for what you already hold
- Keep forms short; depth comes from 
  conversational collection, not surveys

**Collection frequency:** Periodic

---

### 3. One-to-one qualitative conversations

Interviews, coaching sessions, ecosystem discovery 
calls, and informal conversations remain 
irreplaceable for understanding the ecosystem 
at depth. They reveal what no structured source 
can capture: the systemic bottlenecks, the trust 
failures, the relationships that aren't working, 
the demand that exists but has no supply.

**What it captures:**
- Hidden barriers that actors won't write 
  in a survey but will say in conversation
- Systemic patterns observed by practitioners 
  working directly with multiple actors
- Interpretation of weak signals — what a 
  pattern means, not just that it exists
- Relationship maps and informal power 
  structures in the ecosystem

**Who to prioritise:**
Ecosystem operators — accelerators, incubators, 
mentors, event organisers — are particularly 
valuable as conversational sources because they 
observe multiple actors simultaneously. Their 
pattern recognition across a portfolio or 
community is a form of pre-aggregated signal.

**Collection frequency:** Periodic and targeted — 
structured sensing programs after major events, 
program cycles, or when weak signals suggest 
something significant is emerging.

---

### 4. Event-based observation and interaction capture

Events are among the densest moments of ecosystem 
activity — high concentrations of actors, real-time 
intent, and emergent behaviour that doesn't appear 
in any database. A well-instrumented event is a 
major signal collection opportunity.

**What it captures:**
- What questions people ask — especially 
  repeated questions, which indicate systemic gaps
- Who meets whom — network formation and 
  relationship signals
- Which topics, sessions, or speakers trigger 
  the most engagement
- What problems remain unresolved after the event — 
  often the clearest demand signal available
- Demand clusters — groups of actors with 
  similar needs who don't yet know each other

**Design principle:**
Event signals are episodic. They are most valuable 
when they feed a continuous system — not when they 
exist as standalone observations that never connect 
to the broader ecosystem picture.

**Collection frequency:** Event-triggered, feeding 
continuous operational signal layer.

---

### 5. Platform integrations and automated data feeds

The most scalable collection mechanism — and the 
only one that operates at ecosystem scale without 
proportional human effort. Once integrated, 
digital touchpoints generate continuous operational 
signals at low marginal cost.

**What it captures:**
- Applications submitted and their characteristics
- Referrals made and their outcomes
- Meetings booked and participation patterns
- Program engagement and completion
- Funding data and investment activity
- Company registration and lifecycle data
- Knowledge base and platform search behaviour — 
  what actors are looking for and not finding
- AI assistant conversations — one of the richest 
  emerging sources of real-time ecosystem demand signals

**Critical dependency:**
Automated data feeds are only as useful as the 
data model and interoperability design behind them. 
A well-integrated feed into a poorly designed 
taxonomy produces noise, not intelligence. 
The collection layer must be built on the 
signal schemas defined in the 
[Signal Detection & Measurement](./signal-detection-and-measurement.md) 
document.

**Collection frequency:** Continuous

## Matching Method to Signal Type

| Signal type | Best collection method | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Founder needs and blockers | Conversational collection | Continuous |
| Support demand and gaps | Conversational + platform logs | Continuous |
| Emerging topics and trends | Event observation + conversational | Continuous |
| Actor profiles and classification | Structured forms | Periodic |
| Program satisfaction | Surveys | Periodic |
| Ecosystem health indicators | Structured surveys + data feeds | Periodic |
| Partnerships formed | Event-triggered capture | Event-driven |
| Funding decisions | Automated feeds + databases | Event-driven |
| Program completion outcomes | Structured forms | Event-driven |
| Corporate pilot readiness | Platform feeds + conversational | Continuous |
| Investor deployment signals | Automated feeds + intermediary network | Continuous |
| Policy and program availability | Automated feeds + structured | Periodic |

## The Three-Part Collection System

For a territory-level ecosystem, the most effective 
collection design is a three-part system operating 
simultaneously:

### Part 1 — Always-on signal capture
Digital touchpoints, AI assistants, service 
interactions, platform logs, referral flows, 
and event interaction capture. This is the 
continuous operational layer — the ecosystem's 
nervous system. It provides real-time awareness 
of what is happening as it happens.

### Part 2 — Periodic structured measurement
Ecosystem surveys, service inventory updates, 
actor classification, KPI tracking, and outcome 
reviews. This is the structured measurement 
layer — producing comparable data across time 
and enabling trend analysis and benchmark 
comparison.

### Part 3 — Targeted qualitative sensing
Interviews, focus groups, expert conversations, 
and operator debriefs after programs and events. 
This is the interpretation layer — providing 
the qualitative depth needed to understand 
what the operational and measurement layers 
are actually telling you.

Together they provide:
- **Real-time awareness** — from always-on capture
- **Comparable data** — from periodic measurement
- **Qualitative interpretation** — from targeted sensing

No single part is sufficient alone. The intelligence 
comes from combining all three.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Treating collection as reporting**
Designing collection around donor requirements 
and program templates produces data that 
satisfies funders but tells the ecosystem 
operator very little about what is actually 
happening. Collection must be designed 
for operational use, not compliance.

**Asking too much too early**
Long forms, detailed assessments, and quarterly 
update requirements create friction that 
destroys participation. The principle is: 
capture small signals often, collect in context, 
and ask only what creates clear value for 
the actor being asked.

**Confusing activity data with ecosystem understanding**
Counting participants, workshops, applications, 
and events measures motion — not function. 
An ecosystem can be highly active and deeply 
fragmented simultaneously. The collection 
layer must capture what actors are struggling 
with, where support gaps exist, where referrals 
fail, and what produces outcomes — not just 
what is happening at the surface.

**Collecting without a shared taxonomy**
Data collected across actors, programs, and 
time periods is only useful if it can be 
compared and aggregated. Without a shared 
signal taxonomy — consistent definitions 
of stages, sectors, needs categories, 
and actor types — collection produces 
silos in a different format.

**Collecting only from actors already inside programs**
The actors most in need of ecosystem support 
are often the ones least likely to be inside 
existing programs. A collection layer that 
only captures signals from program participants 
creates a systematically biased picture of 
the ecosystem.

## The Natural Rhythm Principle

Rather than asking "should this signal be 
collected continuously or periodically?", 
the better question is:

*What is the natural rhythm of this signal, 
and how close to the source can we capture it?*

A founder's immediate need has a natural rhythm 
of days or weeks — it must be captured in context 
or it is lost. An ecosystem's strategic health 
indicators have a natural rhythm of quarters 
or years — periodic collection is appropriate.

Designing collection around the natural rhythm 
of each signal type produces a system that 
works with how the ecosystem actually moves, 
rather than imposing an institutional 
reporting calendar on it.

## Connection to Other Layers

The collection layer does not operate in isolation:

- It depends on the **Sharing Layer** — actors 
  must be willing and able to share before 
  collection can function
- It feeds the **Aggregation Layer** — raw signals 
  collected here become the input for sense-making
- It is guided by the **Signal Detection & 
  Measurement** framework — which defines what 
  to look for, where to find it, and how to 
  measure it for each actor type
- It is governed by the **Governance Model** — 
  the node operator is responsible for designing 
  and operating the collection system within 
  the data sovereignty framework
