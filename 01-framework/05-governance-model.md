# Governance Model

## Why governance comes first

Most frameworks treat governance as an afterthought — something to figure out once the technology is working. This framework treats it as a prerequisite.

The Ecosystem Intelligence Infrastructure will not be built by a single organization. It will not be owned by a single organization. And it will not serve the interests of a single organization. That is the point. But it also means the hardest questions have to be answered upfront: who makes decisions, who is accountable, who funds the foundational layer, and how does the framework evolve without being captured by any one actor.

Without answers to these questions, the framework is just a design. With them, it becomes something that can actually be built and sustained.

---

## The root cause of the problem

The infrastructure described in this framework doesn't exist — not because the technology is too complex, and not because the need isn't real. It doesn't exist because no actor has been mandated or sufficiently motivated to build and sustain it.

Each ecosystem actor manages their own data, their own tools, and their own programs. The fragmentation is not accidental. It reflects a structural reality: the benefits of shared infrastructure are diffused across many actors, but the cost of building and maintaining it falls on whoever steps up first. In the absence of a mandated operator, no one steps up. Or someone does, builds something, loses funding, and the knowledge disappears with the program.

This is a classic coordination problem. The governance model exists to solve it.

## Core governance principles

Because EII is an open standard intended for adoption across very different contexts — cities, regions, national ecosystems, emerging and developed economies — the governance model is defined by principles rather than by a prescribed institutional structure. What works in Helsinki will not necessarily work in Hanoi. Local adopters are expected to implement governance structures appropriate to their own legal, cultural, and institutional environment.

The following principles apply regardless of local context:

**Federated sovereignty.** Data belongs to the actors who generate it. No central authority holds or controls ecosystem data. Participating nodes operate independently and share what they choose to share, under terms they control.

**No single point of control.** No single organization — public or private — should have unilateral authority over the framework's standards, data models, or decision-making processes. Governance is distributed by design.

**Public interest over commercial interest.** The foundational infrastructure layer exists to serve ecosystem health, not to generate returns for any one actor. Commercial participation is welcome at the service layer. It should not drive decisions about the shared foundation.

**Accountability without centralization.** The framework needs mechanisms for accountability — nodes that misuse data, actors that free-ride, institutions that capture the infrastructure for narrow purposes. These mechanisms should be enforceable without creating central authorities that replicate the control problems they are meant to prevent.

**Continuity across political cycles.** One of the most destructive patterns in public innovation programs is that they disappear when funding cycles end or governments change. The governance model must build in continuity mechanisms that outlast any single political term or funding round.

## Governance structure

EII operates on a federated multi-stakeholder model, drawing on the logic of frameworks like GAIA-X and the European Data Spaces initiative — where standards are held in common, sovereignty remains local, and no single actor controls the shared layer.

In practice, this means:

**Infrastructure stewards** maintain the open standard itself — the data models, the interoperability protocols, the signal taxonomies, the framework documentation. In the EII context, this role is currently held by the open-source community building the framework. As adoption scales, stewardship may transition to a formal body — a foundation, a consortium of ecosystem organizations, or a multi-stakeholder council.

**Node operators** are the organizations that deploy EII within their ecosystems — a regional development agency, a national innovation foundation, a city economic development body. Node operators are responsible for governance within their jurisdiction: who participates, what data is shared, how sovereignty rules are implemented locally.

**Contributing actors** are the ecosystem participants who generate and share signals — startups, incubators, universities, investors, corporations, public institutions. They participate under the terms set by their node operator, with data sovereignty protections guaranteed by the framework.

**Governance council** (at the framework level): as the community grows, a lightweight governance council should emerge — composed of node operators and significant contributors — responsible for stewarding the evolution of the standard, resolving disputes, and preventing capture. Composition, decision rules, and voting thresholds should reflect local norms where possible, while protecting the core principles above.

---

## Decision-making

Decisions about the EII framework fall into three categories:

**Framework evolution** — changes to core data models, signal taxonomies, interoperability standards, or governance principles. These require broad consensus among node operators and significant contributors. No single actor can unilaterally change the shared foundation.

**Local implementation** — how a specific node operates, what data it collects, which actors participate, how funding is structured. These decisions are fully local. Node operators have sovereignty over their implementation as long as they respect the core framework principles.

**Dispute resolution** — when conflicts arise between nodes, between actors and their node operator, or between contributors and the stewardship body. Resolution processes should be defined at the node level for local disputes, and at the framework level for disputes that cross nodes or threaten the integrity of the shared standard. The framework will publish a reference dispute resolution process; local implementations may adapt it.

## Funding model

This is where most discussions of open infrastructure get evasive. The EII framework does not.

The foundational layer of this infrastructure is closer to public infrastructure than to a software product. Roads, ports, electricity grids, and public research infrastructure share a common characteristic: their benefits are systemic and distributed, no single actor captures the full return, and markets alone consistently underinvest in them. Ecosystem intelligence infrastructure has the same structure.

The organizations that benefit from EII include startups, incubators, universities, investors, corporations, public development agencies, and governments. Each captures part of the value. None captures all of it. That is exactly the condition under which public funding is structurally justified — not as charity, but because the foundational layer creates public-good effects, network effects, and long-term returns that market logic alone will not produce.

A territory that wants a stronger innovation economy should not only fund startups. It should also fund the infrastructure that makes startup formation and growth more efficient, more legible, and more connected.

That said, public funding does not mean government builds everything. That is where many initiatives get it wrong. The appropriate public role is:

- Convener of ecosystem actors around a shared standard
- Setter of interoperability requirements
- Initial funder of the foundational layer
- Anchor customer for early deployments
- Governance owner of the shared layer — not the service layer

The actual components of EII — the tools, dashboards, matching engines, analytics applications built on top of the shared infrastructure — can and should involve private vendors, startups, universities, ecosystem organizations, and open-source communities. The framework deliberately separates the infrastructure layer from the service layer to make this possible.

### Recommended funding structure

| Layer | Funding source |
|---|---|
| Foundational shared layer (open standard, core data models, interoperability protocols, governance infrastructure) | Public or donor-backed funding |
| Advanced modules (ecosystem-specific deployments, analytics capabilities, integration services) | Institutional subscriptions or service contracts |
| Service-layer applications (specialized tools, commercial analytics, matchmaking engines) | Private-sector participation |
| Expertise, data, hosting | In-kind contributions from ecosystem organizations |

The common rails are publicly justified. The applications on top can be commercial. The worst outcomes are at the extremes: expecting the private market to build and sustain what is structurally a public good, or making this a rigid government IT project with no ecosystem flexibility or adaptability.

## Accountability mechanisms

Principles without enforcement are declarations. The framework defines the following accountability mechanisms:

**Data sovereignty enforcement.** Node operators must implement and enforce the data sovereignty rules defined in the framework. Contributing actors retain the right to audit what data is held, how it is used, and to withdraw participation. Node operators who violate sovereignty rules are subject to removal from the federated network.

**Transparency requirements.** Node operators publish what data they collect, how it is aggregated, who has access, and under what terms. This is not optional — it is a condition of participation in the federated network.

**Free-rider prevention.** Actors who consume ecosystem intelligence without contributing to it undermine the network's integrity. Node operators are responsible for maintaining contribution standards within their jurisdictions. The framework provides reference guidelines; implementation is local.

**Framework integrity protection.** The stewardship body is responsible for identifying and responding to attempts to capture or distort the shared standard for narrow interests — commercial, political, or institutional. The open-source and public-documentation model is itself a protection mechanism: the framework belongs to no one, which means it cannot be owned by anyone.

## Local adoption

The framework is designed to be adopted by ecosystems operating under very different conditions — different legal systems, different institutional structures, different political economies, different levels of digital maturity.

Local adopters are not expected to replicate a single governance model. They are expected to implement the core principles in ways that work within their context. A regional development agency in Finland will implement EII governance differently than a national innovation foundation in Vietnam, and both implementations can be valid.

What the framework requires of any local adoption:

- Explicit commitment to the core governance principles (federated sovereignty, no single point of control, public interest primacy, accountability, continuity)
- A defined node operator — an organization that accepts responsibility for local governance
- A documented local governance structure — who decides what, how disputes are resolved, how the node is funded
- Participation in the broader federated network under the framework's shared standards

The framework provides reference governance templates to support local adaptation. These are starting points, not mandates.
