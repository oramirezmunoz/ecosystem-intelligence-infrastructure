# Layer 5: Governance Model

## Why governance comes first

Most frameworks treat governance as an afterthought — something 
to figure out once the technology is working. This framework 
treats it as a prerequisite.

The Ecosystem Intelligence Infrastructure will not be built by 
a single organization. It will not be owned by a single 
organization. And it will not serve the interests of a single 
organization. That is the point. But it also means the hardest 
questions have to be answered upfront: who makes decisions, who 
is accountable, who funds the foundational layer, and how does 
the framework evolve without being captured by any one actor.

Without answers to these questions, the framework is just a 
design. With them, it becomes something that can actually be 
built and sustained.

---

## The root cause of the problem

The infrastructure described in this framework doesn't exist — 
not because the technology is too complex, and not because the 
need isn't real. It doesn't exist because no actor has been 
mandated or sufficiently motivated to build and sustain it.

Each ecosystem actor manages their own data, their own tools, 
and their own programs. The fragmentation is not accidental. 
It reflects a structural reality: the benefits of shared 
infrastructure are diffused across many actors, but the cost 
of building and maintaining it falls on whoever steps up first. 
In the absence of a mandated operator, no one steps up. Or 
someone does, builds something, loses funding, and the knowledge 
disappears with the program.

This is a classic coordination problem. The governance model 
exists to solve it.

---

## Core governance principles

Because EII is an open standard intended for adoption across 
very different contexts — cities, regions, national ecosystems, 
emerging and developed economies — the governance model is 
defined by principles rather than by a prescribed institutional 
structure. What works in Helsinki will not necessarily work in 
Hanoi. Local adopters are expected to implement governance 
structures appropriate to their own legal, cultural, and 
institutional environment.

The following principles apply regardless of local context:

**Federated sovereignty.** Data belongs to the actors who 
generate it. No central authority holds or controls ecosystem 
data. Participating nodes operate independently and share what 
they choose to share, under terms they control.

**No single point of control.** No single organization — public 
or private — should have unilateral authority over the 
framework's standards, data models, or decision-making 
processes. Governance is distributed by design.

**Public interest over commercial interest.** The foundational 
infrastructure layer exists to serve ecosystem health, not to 
generate returns for any one actor. Commercial participation 
is welcome at the service layer. It should not drive decisions 
about the shared foundation.

**Accountability without centralization.** The framework needs 
mechanisms for accountability — nodes that misuse data, actors 
that free-ride, institutions that capture the infrastructure 
for narrow purposes. These mechanisms should be enforceable 
without creating central authorities that replicate the control 
problems they are meant to prevent.

**Continuity across political cycles.** One of the most 
destructive patterns in public innovation programs is that 
they disappear when funding cycles end or governments change. 
The governance model must build in continuity mechanisms that 
outlast any single political term or funding round.

## Governance structure

EII operates on a federated multi-stakeholder model, drawing 
on the logic of frameworks like GAIA-X and the European Data 
Spaces initiative — where standards are held in common, 
sovereignty remains local, and no single actor controls 
the shared layer.

In practice, this means:

**Infrastructure stewards** maintain the open standard itself — 
the data models, the interoperability protocols, the signal 
taxonomies, the framework documentation. In the EII context, 
this role is currently held by the open-source community 
building the framework. As adoption scales, stewardship may 
transition to a formal body — a foundation, a consortium of 
ecosystem organizations, or a multi-stakeholder council.

**Node operators** are the organizations that deploy EII within 
their ecosystems — a regional development agency, a national 
innovation foundation, a city economic development body. Node 
operators are responsible for governance within their 
jurisdiction: who participates, what data is shared, how 
sovereignty rules are implemented locally.

The node operator is also the **neutral operator** described 
in the [Sharing Layer](./00-sharing-layer.md) — the trusted, 
mandate-holding entity with no competitive interest in the 
signals it manages, whose existence is the prerequisite for 
the sharing layer to function. These are the same role. 
Building the node operator is the first act of building EII 
in any ecosystem.

**Contributing actors** are the ecosystem participants who 
generate and share signals — startups, incubators, 
universities, investors, corporations, public institutions. 
They participate under the terms set by their node operator, 
with data sovereignty protections guaranteed by the framework.

**Governance council** (at the framework level): as the 
community grows, a lightweight governance council should 
emerge — composed of node operators and significant 
contributors — responsible for stewarding the evolution 
of the standard, resolving disputes, and preventing capture. 
Composition, decision rules, and voting thresholds should 
reflect local norms where possible, while protecting the 
core principles above.

## Decision-making

Decisions about the EII framework fall into three categories:

**Framework evolution** — changes to core data models, signal 
taxonomies, interoperability standards, or governance 
principles. These require broad consensus among node operators 
and significant contributors. No single actor can unilaterally 
change the shared foundation.

**Local implementation** — how a specific node operates, what 
data it collects, which actors participate, how funding is 
structured. These decisions are fully local. Node operators 
have sovereignty over their implementation as long as they 
respect the core framework principles.

**Dispute resolution** — when conflicts arise between nodes, 
between actors and their node operator, or between 
contributors and the stewardship body. Resolution processes 
should be defined at the node level for local disputes, and 
at the framework level for disputes that cross nodes or 
threaten the integrity of the shared standard. The framework 
will publish a reference dispute resolution process; local 
implementations may adapt it.

## Funding model

This is where most discussions of open infrastructure get 
evasive. The EII framework does not.

The foundational layer of this infrastructure is closer to 
public infrastructure than to a software product. Roads, 
ports, electricity grids, and public research infrastructure 
share a common characteristic: their benefits are systemic 
and distributed, no single actor captures the full return, 
and markets alone consistently underinvest in them. Ecosystem 
intelligence infrastructure has the same structure.

The organizations that benefit from EII include startups, 
incubators, universities, investors, corporations, public 
development agencies, and governments. Each captures part 
of the value. None captures all of it. That is exactly the 
condition under which public funding is structurally 
justified — not as charity, but because the foundational 
layer creates public-good effects, network effects, and 
long-term returns that market logic alone will not produce.

A territory that wants a stronger innovation economy should 
not only fund startups. It should also fund the infrastructure 
that makes startup formation and growth more efficient, more 
legible, and more connected.

That said, public funding does not mean government builds 
everything. That is where many initiatives get it wrong. 
The appropriate public role is:

- Convener of ecosystem actors around a shared standard
- Setter of interoperability requirements
- Initial funder of the foundational layer
- Anchor customer for early deployments
- Governance owner of the shared layer — not the service layer

The actual components of EII — the tools, dashboards, 
matching engines, analytics applications built on top of 
the shared infrastructure — can and should involve private 
vendors, startups, universities, ecosystem organizations, 
and open-source communities. The framework deliberately 
separates the infrastructure layer from the service layer 
to make this possible.

### Recommended funding structure

| Layer | Funding source |
|---|---|
| Foundational shared layer (open standard, core data models, interoperability protocols, governance infrastructure) | Public or donor-backed funding |
| Advanced modules (ecosystem-specific deployments, analytics capabilities, integration services) | Institutional subscriptions or service contracts |
| Service-layer applications (specialized tools, commercial analytics, matchmaking engines) | Private-sector participation |
| Expertise, data, hosting | In-kind contributions from ecosystem organizations |

The common rails are publicly justified. The applications 
on top can be commercial. The worst outcomes are at the 
extremes: expecting the private market to build and sustain 
what is structurally a public good, or making this a rigid 
government IT project with no ecosystem flexibility 
or adaptability.

## Accountability mechanisms

Principles without enforcement are declarations. The framework 
defines the following accountability mechanisms:

**Data sovereignty enforcement.** Node operators must implement 
and enforce the data sovereignty rules defined in the 
framework. Contributing actors retain the right to audit what 
data is held, how it is used, and to withdraw participation. 
Node operators who violate sovereignty rules are subject to 
removal from the federated network.

**Transparency requirements.** Node operators publish what 
data they collect, how it is aggregated, who has access, and 
under what terms. This is not optional — it is a condition 
of participation in the federated network.

**Free-rider prevention.** Actors who consume ecosystem 
intelligence without contributing to it undermine the 
network's integrity. Node operators are responsible for 
maintaining contribution standards within their 
jurisdictions. The framework provides reference guidelines; 
implementation is local.

**Framework integrity protection.** The stewardship body is 
responsible for identifying and responding to attempts to 
capture or distort the shared standard for narrow interests — 
commercial, political, or institutional. The open-source and 
public-documentation model is itself a protection mechanism: 
the framework belongs to no one, which means it cannot be 
owned by anyone.

## Minimum viable governance

Not every ecosystem will have the institutional capacity to 
implement a full governance council from day one. The 
following is the absolute minimum governance structure 
required to start a legitimate EII node:

**1. A designated node operator**
A single organization that accepts formal responsibility 
for operating the infrastructure locally. It must have 
no competitive interest in the signals it manages, a 
credible relationship with key ecosystem actors, and 
a sustainable funding commitment for at least 
an initial period.

**2. A documented data sovereignty policy**
A written commitment to the data sovereignty principle — 
what data is collected, how it is used, who can access 
it, and how contributing actors can withdraw. This does 
not need to be complex. It needs to exist and be public.

**3. A defined set of initial contributing actors**
At least three actor types represented from the 
[Actor Taxonomy](../00-foundation/actor-taxonomy.md) — 
with explicit consent to share signals under the 
node's sovereignty policy.

**4. A basic accountability mechanism**
A named contact for disputes, a defined review 
process for sovereignty violations, and a 
commitment to publish an annual transparency 
report on what data was collected, shared, 
and used.

These four elements are the minimum viable governance 
structure. Everything else in this document represents 
the mature state that nodes should evolve toward — 
not a prerequisite for starting.

## Transition pathway: from initiation to 
institutionalisation

Governance does not arrive fully formed. It develops 
in stages as the node matures, trust is established, 
and the value of the infrastructure becomes visible 
to ecosystem actors. The following pathway describes 
how most EII nodes can expect to evolve:

### Stage 1 — Initiation
*Timeframe: months 1 to 6*

A credible anchor institution — typically a public 
agency, innovation foundation, or cluster organisation — 
steps forward as the provisional node operator. 
Minimum viable governance is implemented. 
The first contributing actors are onboarded 
under explicit sovereignty agreements. 
The focus is on demonstrating value, 
not on building comprehensive governance.

### Stage 2 — Validation
*Timeframe: months 6 to 18*

The node has demonstrated measurable value to 
contributing actors. Reciprocity is visible — 
actors can see what their sharing produced. 
A wider set of actor types is onboarded. 
The governance structure is formalised — 
a basic advisory group representing 
contributing actors is established. 
Funding beyond the initial anchor is secured.

### Stage 3 — Institutionalisation
*Timeframe: months 18 to 36*

The node operator has a permanent mandate 
and sustainable multi-source funding. 
A formal governance council is established 
with representation from all major actor types. 
The node participates in the broader EII 
federated network. Transparency reporting 
is published regularly. The infrastructure 
is treated as permanent — not as a project.

### Stage 4 — Maturity
*Timeframe: beyond month 36*

The node is self-sustaining. Governance is 
distributed across multiple institutions. 
The node contributes actively to framework 
evolution. Service-layer applications built 
on top of the shared infrastructure are 
operational. The ecosystem has a functioning, 
permanent intelligence layer.

## Local adoption

The framework is designed to be adopted by ecosystems 
operating under very different conditions — different 
legal systems, different institutional structures, 
different political economies, different levels of 
digital maturity.

Local adopters are not expected to replicate a single 
governance model. They are expected to implement the 
core principles in ways that work within their context. 
A regional development agency in Finland will implement 
EII governance differently than a national innovation 
foundation in Vietnam, and both implementations can 
be valid.

What the framework requires of any local adoption:

- Explicit commitment to the core governance principles 
  (federated sovereignty, no single point of control, 
  public interest primacy, accountability, continuity)
- A defined node operator — an organization that accepts 
  responsibility for local governance
- A documented local governance structure — who decides 
  what, how disputes are resolved, how the node is funded
- Participation in the broader federated network under 
  the framework's shared standards

The framework provides reference governance templates 
to support local adaptation. These are starting points, 
not mandates.
