# Actor Taxonomy

## Reference Framework

This taxonomy uses the **Startup Commons Startup Development Phases** 
as its reference framework. The framework describes the startup journey 
across three main phases:

- **Formation** — inspiring and attracting talent, building 
  entrepreneurial mindset, ideation, early team formation
- **Validation** — initial product development, customer discovery, 
  team strengthening, business model validation
- **Growth / Scale Up** — additional resourcing, scaling operations, 
  international expansion, processes and KPIs

These phases run along two parallel tracks: from *idea to business* 
and from *talent to organization*. Understanding which actors are 
most active in which phases is essential for designing how information 
flows through the ecosystem.

---

## How to Read This Taxonomy

For each actor type, this document describes:
- **Role in the ecosystem** — what they do and why they matter
- **Most active phases** — where they concentrate their activity 
  relative to the startup development journey
- **Signals they generate** — what information they hold that 
  is valuable to the rest of the ecosystem
- **Why they don't share** — the specific barriers to sharing 
  for this actor type
- **What unlocks sharing** — the combination of incentives, 
  governance, and trust conditions that make sharing possible

## 1. Public Entities

*City, regional, and national bodies with competencies and 
responsibilities to develop the innovation and entrepreneurship 
ecosystem.*

### Role in the ecosystem
The primary funders and policy shapers of the ecosystem. They 
design instruments, allocate budgets, and set the regulatory 
environment that enables or constrains all other actors.

### Most active phases
All phases — but most intensively in **Formation** through 
education and awareness policy, and in **Validation** through 
grant programs and support structures.

### Signals they generate
- Policy priorities and upcoming regulatory changes
- Budget allocations for innovation and entrepreneurship programs
- Program performance data — what is working and what is not
- Economic and labour market data at city, regional, and 
  national level
- Public procurement opportunities relevant to startups
- Strategic development plans and roadmaps

### Why they don't share
- Program performance data carries political exposure
- Bureaucratic silos — different agencies hold different 
  data with no internal mandate to consolidate
- Legal and compliance constraints on data sharing
- Slow institutional decision cycles
- Low awareness that their data has operational value 
  for other ecosystem actors

### What unlocks sharing
- **Mandate:** the most reliable unlock — public entities 
  share when they are formally required or encouraged to 
  do so through policy design
- **Framing:** sharing as a tool to improve the effectiveness 
  of public investment, which is already their stated mandate
- **Governance:** inter-institutional data sharing agreements 
  that make sharing legally safe
- **Anonymization:** program performance data can often be 
  shared in aggregate without political exposure

## 2. Universities

### Role in the ecosystem
Generators of research, technology, and talent. Key actors in 
connecting scientific knowledge with entrepreneurial 
application — but often operating in institutional silos 
that limit that connection.

### Most active phases
**Formation** (talent pipeline, entrepreneurship education) and 
**Validation** (research commercialisation, spin-offs, 
early technology development).

### Signals they generate
- Research outputs and technology readiness levels
- Researcher expertise and collaboration appetite
- Spin-off and licensing opportunities
- Student and graduate talent availability
- Infrastructure and equipment available for industry use
- Funded research programs with open partnership slots

### Why they don't share
- IP protection — premature disclosure can affect patent 
  filing and commercialisation rights
- Institutional bureaucracy — technology transfer offices 
  are often slow and risk-averse
- Academic incentive structures don't reward industry 
  engagement in many systems
- Poor awareness of what startups and industry actually need
- Internal silos between faculties and departments

### What unlocks sharing
- **Incentives:** industry partnerships, funded research 
  collaborations, and visibility for researchers and 
  institutions — sharing leads to partnerships, not 
  just exposure
- **Governance:** clear IP frameworks established upfront 
  so researchers know what they can share and when
- **Translation:** research signals need packaging in 
  language that startups and industry understand — 
  raw academic output is rarely actionable without 
  interpretation
- **Intermediaries:** technology transfer offices and 
  knowledge brokers play a critical bridging role

## 3. Coworking Spaces

### Role in the ecosystem
Physical infrastructure for early-stage entrepreneurs and 
remote workers. Often the first point of contact with the 
ecosystem for new entrepreneurs, and an important node 
for informal knowledge exchange.

### Most active phases
**Formation** and early **Validation** — where founders need 
affordable space, community, and first connections.

### Signals they generate
- Volume and profile of entrepreneurs in their community
- Skill sets and sector concentrations in their member base
- Collaboration needs and team formation opportunities
- Early signals of what entrepreneurs are building
- Space and infrastructure availability

### Why they don't share
- Member privacy — entrepreneurs sharing a workspace 
  have not necessarily consented to being visible 
  in the wider ecosystem
- Commercial sensitivity — member data is a business asset
- Limited internal capacity for data management
- No clear incentive to invest time in sharing

### What unlocks sharing
- **Consent frameworks:** members opt in to ecosystem 
  visibility as part of membership
- **Incentives:** access to relevant programs, events, 
  and connections for their members in return for 
  aggregated data
- **Simplicity:** lightweight sharing mechanisms that 
  require minimal operational effort
- **Aggregation:** community-level signals (sector 
  concentrations, skill availability) shared without 
  identifying individuals

## 4. Startup Event Organisers

### Role in the ecosystem
Builders of community and visibility. Events are among the 
most effective mechanisms for connecting actors across 
the ecosystem and for surfacing emerging trends and talent.

### Most active phases
Active across all phases — but particularly important in 
**Formation** for inspiration and awareness, and in 
**Validation** for pitching, feedback, and connection.

### Signals they generate
- Participation data — who is engaging with the ecosystem 
  and at what stage
- Emerging topics and themes — what the ecosystem is 
  talking about
- Talent signals — who is showing up, pitching, 
  and looking for connections
- Network gaps — who is not being reached

### Why they don't share
- Attendee data is commercially and competitively sensitive
- Events are often one-off — no systematic data 
  collection or retention practice
- No perceived incentive to share beyond their own 
  promotional interests

### What unlocks sharing
- **Reciprocity:** access to ecosystem-wide data that 
  helps them design better, more relevant events
- **Aggregation:** participation trends and topic 
  signals shared without individual attendee data
- **Collaboration:** joint event formats with other 
  ecosystem actors that create natural data sharing 
  as a by-product

## 5. Incubators

### Role in the ecosystem
Structured support providers for early-stage startups. 
Typically focused on Formation and early Validation — 
helping founders build the foundations of a viable 
business before they are ready for acceleration.

### Most active phases
**Formation** and early **Validation.**

### Signals they generate
- Portfolio company profiles and development stages
- Program design — what support is working and what is not
- Common challenges faced by early-stage founders
- Mentor and expert network availability
- Demand signals from founders — what they need 
  and cannot find

### Why they don't share
- Portfolio data belongs to the startups — cannot share 
  without explicit consent
- Competitive dynamics with other incubators competing 
  for the same founders and funders
- Program effectiveness data may reveal underperformance
- Limited data management capacity

### What unlocks sharing
- **Consent frameworks:** startups opt in to ecosystem 
  visibility as part of program participation
- **Collective governance:** incubators share more when 
  they co-govern the infrastructure alongside other 
  operators, with no single player gaining advantage
- **Reciprocity:** a richer view of the full ecosystem 
  in return for contributing their slice
- **Aggregation:** portfolio signals at cohort or 
  sector level without identifying individual startups

## 6. Accelerators

### Role in the ecosystem
Intensive, time-bounded programs that help validated 
startups grow faster — typically through mentorship, 
investment, and corporate connections.

### Most active phases
**Validation** (late) and **Growth / Scale Up** (early).

### Signals they generate
- Portfolio company traction and growth metrics
- Corporate and investor demand — what partners 
  are actively looking for
- Investment readiness signals across the portfolio
- Program performance and outcomes data
- Market signals from deal flow and corporate engagement

### Why they don't share
- Portfolio data is commercially sensitive — traction 
  metrics are a fundraising asset
- Corporate partner relationships are proprietary 
  and not for sharing
- Competitive dynamics between accelerators are 
  particularly intense
- Program outcomes data may attract unwanted scrutiny 
  from funders

### What unlocks sharing
- **Governance:** strict controls on how signals 
  are used — corporate and investor relationships 
  must be protected
- **Reciprocity:** access to better deal flow, 
  stronger mentor networks, and corporate connections 
  from the broader ecosystem
- **Collective programs:** co-organised cohorts and 
  shared selection processes create natural conditions 
  for sharing
- **Aggregated outcomes:** sector-level performance 
  data shared without identifying individual programs 
  or portfolio companies

## 7. Tech Parks

### Role in the ecosystem
Physical and institutional anchors for innovation — 
providing infrastructure, hosting companies at various 
stages, and often acting as a bridge between research 
institutions and industry.

### Most active phases
**Validation** and **Growth / Scale Up** — where companies 
need stable infrastructure, proximity to research, 
and connections to industry.

### Signals they generate
- Tenant company profiles and sector concentrations
- Infrastructure and facility availability
- Research-industry collaboration activity
- Foreign direct investment attraction signals
- Cluster formation and specialisation trends

### Why they don't share
- Tenant data is commercially sensitive
- Competition with other tech parks for tenants 
  and investors
- Governance structures often limit what can 
  be shared publicly

### What unlocks sharing
- **Collective positioning:** tech parks share when 
  it strengthens the overall ecosystem reputation, 
  which benefits all of them
- **Aggregation:** cluster-level data shared without 
  tenant-specific information
- **Governance:** formal participation in EII 
  governance gives them confidence in how 
  data is used

---

## 8. Investors

### Role in the ecosystem
Capital providers and growth catalysts. Their decisions 
about where to invest shape the trajectory of the 
ecosystem as much as any policy instrument.

### Most active phases
**Validation** (seed and early stage) through 
**Growth / Scale Up** (Series A and beyond).

### Signals they generate
- Active investment thesis and focus areas
- Stage and ticket size preferences
- Portfolio gaps and co-investment appetite
- Market signals derived from deal flow
- Assessment of ecosystem maturity and gaps
- Exit activity and returns data

### Why they don't share
- Deal flow is a competitive advantage
- Investment thesis is often deliberately opaque 
  to avoid being gamed
- Relationships are currency — fear of 
  disintermediation
- Portfolio performance data is strictly private

### What unlocks sharing
- **Incentives:** access to better, pre-qualified 
  deal flow — investors share when the return 
  is higher quality opportunities
- **Reciprocity:** structured peer exchange among 
  investors where everyone contributes and 
  everyone benefits
- **Governance:** strict controls — investment 
  thesis and portfolio data never public
- **Aggregation:** market-level signals (where 
  capital is flowing, which sectors are underserved) 
  shared without individual investor positions

## 9. Banks

### Role in the ecosystem
Providers of debt financing and financial services. 
Often underestimated as ecosystem actors, banks hold 
significant intelligence about the real financial 
health and growth patterns of companies across the 
ecosystem.

### Most active phases
**Growth / Scale Up** — where startups begin to need 
debt instruments, credit lines, and banking 
infrastructure beyond basic accounts.

### Signals they generate
- Demand for financial products among startups 
  and SMEs
- Gaps in available financing instruments
- Financial health patterns across sectors
- Interest in innovation-linked financial products
- Public guarantee program utilisation

### Why they don't share
- Strict financial data confidentiality regulations
- No tradition of participation in ecosystem 
  development conversations
- Internal siloing between commercial and 
  innovation teams
- Competitive sensitivity around product design

### What unlocks sharing
- **Regulatory framing:** participation framed 
  as aligned with financial inclusion or 
  innovation mandates they already carry
- **Aggregation:** sector and stage-level financial 
  demand signals shared without client data
- **Collective interest:** banks benefit when the 
  companies they serve grow — sharing that supports 
  ecosystem health is in their interest
- **Formal engagement:** structured participation 
  in ecosystem governance gives them a legitimate 
  reason to engage

## 10. Mentors

### Role in the ecosystem
Experience carriers. Mentors transfer knowledge, 
networks, and judgment to founders — and represent 
one of the most valuable and most underutilised 
resources in any ecosystem.

### Most active phases
All phases — but most intensively in **Validation**, 
where founder decisions have the highest consequence.

### Signals they generate
- Expertise domains and sector experience
- Availability and mentoring capacity
- Common challenges observed across mentees
- Network connections available to the ecosystem
- Gaps in the mentor pool — expertise areas 
  that are systematically missing

### Why they don't share
- Privacy — mentoring relationships are confidential
- No structured channel for sharing ecosystem 
  observations
- Time constraints — sharing feels like additional 
  overhead
- Underestimate the value of their own signals 
  to the ecosystem

### What unlocks sharing
- **Minimal friction:** lightweight mechanisms — 
  a simple periodic input, not a complex system
- **Recognition:** visibility and acknowledgment 
  within the ecosystem for their contribution
- **Aggregation:** common patterns observed 
  across mentees shared without identifying 
  individual founders
- **Community:** mentor networks that make 
  sharing a social norm, not an administrative task

## 11. Media

*Media organisations supporting innovation and 
entrepreneurship.*

### Role in the ecosystem
Signal amplifiers and narrative shapers. Media 
translates ecosystem activity into stories that 
reach broader audiences — including potential 
founders, investors, corporates, and policymakers 
who would otherwise not be reached.

### Most active phases
Active across all phases — with particular impact 
in **Formation** (inspiring new entrepreneurs) 
and **Growth** (amplifying success stories).

### Signals they generate
- What stories are getting traction — what the 
  broader public finds relevant
- Emerging trends and narratives in the ecosystem
- Which actors are gaining visibility and why
- Gaps — what is happening in the ecosystem 
  that is not being covered

### Why they don't share
- Journalistic independence — being seen as 
  an arm of the ecosystem may compromise credibility
- Competitive dynamics between publications
- Source protection concerns

### What unlocks sharing
- **Editorial framing:** participation framed 
  as access to better stories and ecosystem 
  intelligence, not as a promotional relationship
- **Independence preserved:** EII treats media 
  as an observer and amplifier, not as a 
  managed communications channel
- **Reciprocity:** ecosystem intelligence that 
  helps them identify stories they would 
  otherwise miss

## 12. Chambers of Commerce

### Role in the ecosystem
Connectors between the traditional business 
community and the innovation ecosystem. Chambers 
have access to SME networks that innovation 
actors rarely reach directly.

### Most active phases
Most relevant in **Validation** (connecting 
startups with potential early customers among 
SME members) and **Growth** (market access 
and commercial partnerships).

### Signals they generate
- Collective needs and challenges of SME members
- Openness of member companies to new solutions 
  and partnerships
- Workforce and skills gaps across traditional sectors
- Local market intelligence
- Business community sentiment

### Why they don't share
- Represent member interests — cannot share 
  member-specific data without consent
- Institutional conservatism — sharing outside 
  membership feels like giving away member value
- Limited internal capacity for data management
- Disconnection from the startup ecosystem culture

### What unlocks sharing
- **Collective benefit framing:** sharing ecosystem 
  signals improves conditions for their members — 
  it is an extension of their core mandate
- **Governance:** a seat in EII governance 
  gives them confidence and legitimacy
- **Aggregation:** sector and need-level signals 
  shared without identifying individual members
- **Concrete value:** connecting their members 
  with relevant startup solutions creates 
  immediate, visible benefit

## 13. Corporates

*Large companies with significant impact on the national 
economy and/or with active open innovation plans 
or strategies.*

### Role in the ecosystem
Potential customers, partners, and acquirers for 
startups. When engaged well, corporates are among 
the most powerful accelerants of startup growth — 
providing pilots, contracts, distribution, and 
eventual acquisition exits.

### Most active phases
**Validation** (as early adopters and pilot partners) 
and **Growth / Scale Up** (as commercial partners, 
investors, and acquirers).

### Signals they generate
- Innovation needs and technology gaps
- Openness to piloting startup solutions — 
  the most critical and most invisible signal 
  in most ecosystems
- Strategic priorities and investment areas
- Procurement appetite and budget availability
- Internal R&D activity and spin-off potential
- Talent availability through restructuring

### Why they don't share
- Don't want to be flooded with irrelevant pitches
- Innovation appetite varies by department and 
  is not always sanctioned from the top
- IP and competitive confidentiality concerns
- No internal owner for this type of engagement
- Bureaucratic friction — sharing requires approvals

### What unlocks sharing
- **Incentives:** access to pre-filtered, highly 
  relevant startup solutions — quality over volume
- **Governance:** they define what they share, 
  with whom, and at what level of specificity
- **Anonymization:** initial signals at category 
  level before identity is revealed
- **Trusted intermediary:** a credible ecosystem 
  operator managing the relationship — not a 
  cold platform

---

## 14. Donors & Development Financiers

*Foundations, international development agencies, 
and financiers supporting the innovation and 
entrepreneurship environment.*

### Role in the ecosystem
Providers of grant funding, technical assistance, 
and institutional capacity building — particularly 
important in emerging ecosystems where private 
capital is scarce and public budgets are limited.

### Most active phases
**Formation** (ecosystem building, education, 
awareness) and **Validation** (grant programs, 
challenge funds, early-stage support).

### Signals they generate
- Funding priorities and open calls
- Program performance across funded initiatives
- Cross-ecosystem learning from global portfolios
- Gaps and opportunities they have identified 
  but not yet funded

### Why they don't share
- Grantee confidentiality obligations
- Internal bureaucracy and approval cycles
- Competition between development organisations 
  for visibility and attribution
- Reporting requirements consume capacity 
  that could otherwise go to sharing

### What unlocks sharing
- **Mission alignment:** sharing ecosystem intelligence 
  directly serves their development mandate
- **Aggregation:** portfolio-level insights shared 
  without grantee-specific data
- **Reciprocity:** access to ecosystem intelligence 
  that improves the quality and relevance 
  of their program design
- **Coordination frameworks:** formal coordination 
  mechanisms among donors reduce duplication 
  and create natural sharing infrastructure

## 15. New Entrepreneurs

*Individuals at the beginning of the entrepreneurial 
journey — exploring ideas, forming teams, and 
entering the ecosystem for the first time.*

### Role in the ecosystem
The raw material of the ecosystem. Their volume, 
quality, and diversity determine the long-term 
health of the startup pipeline.

### Most active phases
**Formation** — building entrepreneurial mindset, 
exploring ideas, finding co-founders and mentors.

### Signals they generate
- Entrepreneurial intent and readiness
- Skill sets and sector interests entering 
  the ecosystem
- Barriers to entry — what is stopping potential 
  entrepreneurs from starting
- Support needs — what they are looking for 
  and cannot find

### Why they don't share
- Low awareness that their situation has 
  value to the ecosystem
- Fear of idea theft
- Distrust of institutions
- No structured channel to share even 
  if they wanted to

### What unlocks sharing
- **Safe channels:** low-stakes, trusted environments 
  where sharing feels natural — events, communities, 
  coworking spaces — rather than formal systems
- **Immediate value:** connect them with something 
  useful immediately in return for sharing 
  where they are in their journey
- **Anonymization:** aggregate signals about 
  new entrepreneur profiles and barriers without 
  identifying individuals

## 16. Experienced Entrepreneurs

*Founders who have built and scaled companies — 
including both successes and failures.*

### Role in the ecosystem
Experience multipliers. When actively engaged, 
experienced entrepreneurs are the most credible 
and effective mentors, angel investors, and 
role models in any ecosystem.

### Most active phases
They operate across all phases — but their signal 
value is highest for **Validation** and **Growth**, 
where their pattern recognition is most relevant.

### Signals they generate
- Hard-won knowledge about what works and 
  what doesn't at each startup development phase
- Angel investment appetite and thesis
- Mentoring availability and domain expertise
- Ecosystem gaps they have personally experienced
- Diaspora connections and international networks

### Why they don't share
- Busy building their next venture or 
  managing investments
- Selective about where they invest their time — 
  sharing needs to feel worthwhile
- Reluctance to share failure experience 
  in cultures where failure carries stigma

### What unlocks sharing
- **Recognition and community:** belonging to 
  a peer group of experienced founders 
  creates social incentive to contribute
- **Curated engagement:** they share when 
  engagement is specific and relevant — 
  not generic requests for their time
- **Culture shift:** ecosystems that normalise 
  sharing failure experience unlock the most 
  valuable signals this actor type holds

## 17. Entrepreneurial Associations

*Organised groups representing the interests of 
entrepreneurs and startups at local, regional, 
or national level.*

### Role in the ecosystem
Collective voice of the entrepreneurial community. 
Translate individual founder experiences into 
structured advocacy and collective action.

### Most active phases
Relevant across all phases — particularly important 
in **Formation** (representing conditions for entry) 
and **Growth** (advocating for scale-friendly policy).

### Signals they generate
- Collective needs and challenges of the 
  entrepreneur community
- Regulatory barriers and policy gaps
- Ecosystem sentiment — how founders actually 
  feel about the support available
- Gaps in representation — who is not being 
  heard in ecosystem conversations

### Why they don't share
- Represent member interests — cannot share 
  without member consent
- Limited operational capacity
- Cautious about data being used in ways 
  that don't serve their members

### What unlocks sharing
- **Mandate alignment:** sharing supports 
  their advocacy mission — it gives them 
  better evidence for policy arguments
- **Governance participation:** a seat in 
  EII governance ensures member interests 
  are protected
- **Reciprocity:** ecosystem intelligence 
  that makes them more effective advocates 
  for their members

## 18. Intellectual Property Registration Entity

*The national body responsible for registering 
patents, trademarks, and other IP.*

### Role in the ecosystem
Holder of one of the most underutilised datasets 
in any ecosystem — IP registration data reveals 
where innovation is happening, at what pace, 
and in which sectors, long before it becomes 
commercially visible.

### Most active phases
Most relevant in **Validation** — where technology 
is being defined and protected — and **Growth**, 
where IP becomes a commercial asset.

### Signals they generate
- Patent and trademark filing trends by sector 
  and geography
- Technology areas with growing or declining 
  activity
- Researcher and company innovation output
- International IP filing patterns — who is 
  protecting innovations for global markets

### Why they don't share
- Legal constraints on individual filing data
- No tradition of contributing to ecosystem 
  development conversations
- Institutional conservatism

### What unlocks sharing
- **Mandate:** reframing IP data sharing as 
  a contribution to national economic development — 
  consistent with their institutional purpose
- **Aggregation:** sector and technology-level 
  trends shared without individual filing data
- **Policy alignment:** IP trends are directly 
  relevant to innovation policy — a natural 
  link to public entity ecosystem development mandates

## 19. Professional Service Providers

*Accounting firms, lawyers, and other professional 
services companies providing services to startups.*

### Role in the ecosystem
Operational enablers. Professional service providers 
see startups at their most vulnerable moments — 
incorporation, fundraising, IP protection, 
financial distress — and hold signals about 
ecosystem health that no other actor type accesses.

### Most active phases
**Validation** (incorporation, early contracts, 
IP protection) and **Growth** (fundraising 
structures, employment law, tax optimisation).

### Signals they generate
- Common legal and financial challenges faced 
  by startups at each development phase
- Regulatory barriers encountered in practice
- Volume and quality of company formation activity
- Fundraising deal structures and terms trends

### Why they don't share
- Strict client confidentiality obligations
- No incentive to share — their knowledge 
  is their commercial product
- Legal liability concerns

### What unlocks sharing
- **Aggregation only:** individual client data 
  is never shareable — but anonymised patterns 
  (common legal barriers, financial challenges 
  by stage) can be
- **Community positioning:** being known as 
  ecosystem contributors attracts startup clients
- **Structured engagement:** participation in 
  ecosystem working groups rather than 
  data submission

## 20. Industry Associations

### Role in the ecosystem
Sector-level connectors. Industry associations 
hold collective intelligence about the needs, 
trends, and challenges of their member industries 
that is rarely visible to the innovation ecosystem.

### Most active phases
Most relevant in **Validation** (connecting 
startups with sector problems to solve) and 
**Growth** (market access and commercial 
adoption at scale).

### Signals they generate
- Sector-level challenges and unmet needs
- Workforce and technology gaps
- Regulatory developments affecting the sector
- International market trends
- Member company openness to innovation partnerships

### Why they don't share
- Represent collective member interests — 
  cannot share member-specific data
- Institutional conservatism
- Disconnection from startup ecosystem culture
- Limited internal capacity

### What unlocks sharing
- **Collective benefit framing:** sharing 
  sector intelligence attracts innovation 
  that serves their members
- **Aggregation:** industry-level needs and 
  trends without identifying individual members
- **Governance:** a seat in EII governance 
  ensures their sector's interests are represented

## 21. International Companies Investing in the Country

### Role in the ecosystem
Foreign direct investment (FDI) actors who bring 
capital, technology, talent, and global market 
access to the local ecosystem — and who represent 
a critical signal of ecosystem maturity to 
external audiences.

### Most active phases
**Growth / Scale Up** — where startups and the 
ecosystem are ready for international capital 
and partnership.

### Signals they generate
- Investment criteria and sector focus 
  for the local market
- Technology and talent needs that could 
  be met locally
- Perception of local ecosystem maturity 
  and gaps
- Global market access opportunities for 
  local startups

### Why they don't share
- Investment decisions are commercially sensitive
- Limited engagement with local ecosystem 
  infrastructure — they are often isolated 
  from domestic actors
- Language and cultural barriers

### What unlocks sharing
- **Curated engagement:** targeted, high-quality 
  connections rather than broad ecosystem exposure
- **Reciprocity:** access to pre-filtered local 
  opportunities that match their criteria
- **Trusted intermediary:** a credible local 
  actor managing the relationship

---

## 22. Online Platforms & Portals

*Digital platforms and portals that support 
entrepreneurs and startups.*

### Role in the ecosystem
Digital infrastructure for information access, 
community building, and service delivery. 
Platforms aggregate user behaviour data that 
is among the richest sources of demand-side 
intelligence in any ecosystem.

### Most active phases
Relevant across all phases — different platforms 
serve different stages of the journey.

### Signals they generate
- What entrepreneurs are searching for 
  and not finding
- Most accessed resources and tools 
  by development phase
- User engagement patterns — where founders 
  spend time and where they drop off
- Geographic and sector concentrations 
  of platform users

### Why they don't share
- User data is a core business asset
- Privacy obligations to users
- Competitive dynamics — user intelligence 
  is proprietary

### What unlocks sharing
- **Aggregation:** behavioural patterns and 
  demand signals at ecosystem level without 
  individual user data
- **Partnership models:** co-designed 
  intelligence sharing with ecosystem 
  operators in exchange for distribution 
  and visibility
- **User consent:** opt-in frameworks that 
  let users contribute to ecosystem 
  intelligence explicitly

---

## 23. Collaborative Initiatives

*Organised forms of cooperation between ecosystem 
actors, including:*
- Joint marketing activities
- Service provider tools and shared knowledge
- Meetups between organisations and key people
- Shared processes
- Co-organised events
- Shared budgets (consortiums)
- Shared tools (websites, mailing lists, 
  social media accounts, hashtags)
- Shared open data and metrics

### Role in the ecosystem
Collaborative initiatives are the living proof 
that the ecosystem is more than a collection 
of independent actors. They are both a signal 
of ecosystem maturity and a mechanism for 
accelerating it — because collaboration itself 
generates shared intelligence that no single 
actor could produce alone.

### Most active phases
Relevant across all phases — the nature of 
collaboration shifts from community building 
in **Formation** to operational coordination 
in **Validation** and **Growth**.

### Signals they generate
- Which actors are willing and able to cooperate
- What coordination mechanisms are working in practice
- Shared metrics and open data produced 
  through collective activity
- Gaps in collaboration — where coordination 
  is needed but not happening

### Why they don't share
- Governance complexity — multiple actors 
  must agree on what to share
- Attribution concerns — who gets credit 
  for shared outputs
- Sustainability challenges — collaborative 
  initiatives are often fragile and under-resourced

### What unlocks sharing
- **EII as the natural home:** collaborative 
  initiatives are the most natural contributors 
  to EII because they already operate on 
  shared infrastructure logic
- **Shared metrics by design:** build data 
  sharing into the governance of the 
  initiative from the start
- **Open data as a default:** initiatives 
  that commit to open data from inception 
  generate the most valuable ecosystem signals

## Cross-Cutting Design Principles

**Trust precedes technology**
No actor shares because a platform exists. 
They share because they trust the operator, 
the governance, and the value exchange.

**Reciprocity is the universal incentive**
Across every actor type, the most reliable 
unlock is a clear, tangible return for sharing. 
The infrastructure must be designed so that 
sharing always produces visible value for 
the sharer.

**Intermediaries matter more than systems**
Sharing happens through trusted relationships — 
not cold platforms. EII must work through 
existing trust networks, not replace them.

**Phase-awareness improves relevance**
The signals an actor generates and needs 
change depending on which startup development 
phase they are most active in. EII must be 
designed to route information with phase 
awareness — not just actor awareness.

**Governance must be layered**
A single governance model does not work 
across 23 actor types. The infrastructure 
needs layered governance — different rules 
for different data types and different 
actor relationships.

**Anonymization extends reach**
Many signals that actors are unwilling to 
share at the individual level can be shared 
safely in aggregate. Designing for 
anonymization from the start dramatically 
expands what the infrastructure can collect 
and distribute.
